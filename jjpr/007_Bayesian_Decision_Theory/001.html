When you use "continuous random variable" for cumulitive distribution function, derivative of cumulitive distribution function is "probability density function". ================================================================================ When you deal with continuous random variable, how can you give values to others? x=-1 -> probability value 0.3 x=0 -> probability value 0.2 x=1 -> probability value 0.3 ...? Formulas are created by mathematicians. ================================================================================ This is "univariate Gaussian probability density function"" $$$f_X(X)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\exp \left[ -\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma} \right)^2 \right]$$$ ================================================================================ $$$f_X(X)$$$: univariate X, function f changes wrt X $$$\sigma$$$: standard deviation (\sigma^2 is variance) $$$\mu$$$: expectation value, average $$$e$$$: 2.146... ================================================================================ Simple notation $$$f(x;\mu=0,\sigma^2=4)$$$ Formula notation $$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}2}\exp \left[ -\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{X}{2} \right)^2 \right]$$$ ================================================================================ Univariate Gaussian probability density function has one variable X like this /home/young/Pictures/2019_04_08_07:56:32.png ================================================================================ Multivariate Gaussian probability density function $$$f_X(X)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{\frac{n}{2}}\sqrt{|\Sigma|}} \exp \left[ -\frac{1}{2} (X-\mu)^T\Sigma^{-1}(X-\mu) \right]$$$ $$$n$$$: dimenstion of vectors ================================================================================ $$$X=\begin{bmatrix} x_1\\x_2\\\vdots\\x_N \end{bmatrix}$$$ ================================================================================ $$$\mu=\begin{bmatrix} \mu_1\\\mu_2\\\vdots\\\mu_N \end{bmatrix}$$$ $$$\mu_1$$$: Average from random variable 1 $$$\vdots$$$ $$$\mu_N$$$: Average from random variable N ================================================================================ When you use univariate ramdom variable, you use variance (actually std $$$\sigma$$$ from $$$\sqrt{\sigma^2}$$$). But when you use multivariate ramdom variable, you use covariance $$$\Sigma$$$. $$$\Sigma=\begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1^2 & & & & \\ & \sigma_2^2 & & & \\ & & \cdots & & \\ & & & & \sigma_N^2 \end{bmatrix}$$$ $$$\sigma_1^2$$$: variance of random variable 1 $$$\vdots$$$ $$$\sigma_N^2$$$: variance of random variable N Off-diagonal regions: covariance values ================================================================================ When $$$n=2$$$, for example, random variable1=body weight, random variable2=height /home/young/Pictures/2019_04_08_08:16:22.png ================================================================================ Simple notation $$$f(\begin{bmatrix} x_1\\x_2 \end{bmatrix};\mu=\begin{bmatrix} 1\\2 \end{bmatrix},\Sigma=\begin{bmatrix} 2&&0\\0&&4 \end{bmatrix})$$$ Formula notation $$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{(2\pi)^2}\sqrt{8}}\exp \left[ -\frac{1}{2} \begin{bmatrix} x_1-1&&x_2-2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.5&&0\\0&&0.25 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1-1\\x_2-2 \end{bmatrix} \right]$$$
